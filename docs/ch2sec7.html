Game Design Talks & Videos

1. Mark Brown - Game Maker's Toolkit (YouTube)
  • Why it's great: Mark analyzes game design with incredible clarity. His "Boss Keys" series deconstructs level design in Zelda games, perfect for understanding progression.
  • Recommended videos:

  • Link: Search "Game Maker's Toolkit" on YouTube

2. GDC (Game Developers Conference) Talks
  • Why it's great: Professional developers share postmortems, design philosophies, and technical breakdowns. Free on YouTube.
  • Recommended talks:
  • "Celeste and TowerFall: Designing for Player Trust" by Matt Thorson (feedback and fairness)
  • "Magic: The Gathering - 20 Years, 20 Lessons Learned" by Mark Rosewater (evergreen design principles)
  • "Juice It or Lose It" by Martin Jonasson & Petri Purho (making games feel good)
  • Link: Search "GDC" on YouTube, filter by "Most Popular"

3. Extra Credits (YouTube)
  • Why it's great: Short, animated videos on game design topics. Great for quick learning.
  • Recommended videos:
  • "Perfect Imbalance" (why asymmetry keeps games interesting)

  • "Flow" (difficulty balancing and flow state)
  • Link: Search "Extra Credits" on YouTube

Books on Game Design

4. "The Art of Game Design: A Book of Lenses" by Jesse Schell
  • Why it's great: The definitive game design textbook. 100+ "lenses" (perspectives) for analyzing your game.
  • Best for: Deep dives into design philosophy. Not a quick read, but invaluable long-term.
  • Where to get: Amazon, local library, or eBook

---

5. "Rules of Play" by Katie Salen and Eric Zimmerman
  • Why it's great: Academic but accessible. Covers game systems, player psychology, and design frameworks.
  • Best for: Understanding why games work, not just how to make them.
  • Where to get: Amazon, used bookstores (often cheaper)

---

6. "Level Up! The Guide to Great Video Game Design" by Scott Rogers
  • Why it's great: Practical, beginner-friendly, funny. Covers everything from core mechanics to UI design.
  • Best for: Designers who want actionable advice with humor.
  • Where to get: Amazon, or check your local library

Mechanics Analysis: Learn from Great Games

Want to sharpen your mechanic analysis skills? Play these games critically and ask:

  • What are the core mechanics?
  • How do mechanics interact?
  • How does difficulty progress?
  • What feedback keeps me engaged?

---

Recommended Games to Study (By Genre)

Platformers:
  • Celeste (tight movement, difficulty accessibility)
  • Super Meat Boy (precision platforming, fast retry loop)
  • Hollow Knight (exploration, combat depth)
Puzzle Games:
  • Portal 1 & 2 (single mechanic deeply explored)
  • The Witness (environmental storytelling through puzzles)
  • Baba Is You (rules as mechanics)
Action/Combat:
  • Hades (fast combat, roguelike progression)
  • Doom Eternal (aggressive combat loop)
  • Dark Souls (deliberate combat, learning enemy patterns)
Simulation:
  • Stardew Valley (layered systems, player-driven goals)
  • Cities: Skylines (systems complexity)
  • Factorio (optimization and efficiency loops)
Narrative:
  • Undertale (choice-driven mechanics)
  • What Remains of Edith Finch (environmental storytelling)
  • Disco Elysium (dialogue as mechanics)

Play these games with a designer's mindset. Pause and ask: "Why did the designer make this choice?"

Tips for Moving Forward

Before you dive into Chapter 3, here are some mindset tips:

---

Tip 1: Start with ONE Mechanic

Don't try to build all three core mechanics at once. Pick the simplest (probably movement) and prototype that first. Get it working, get it feeling good, then add the next mechanic.

Why: Building incrementally prevents overwhelm and lets you test as you go.

---

Tip 2: Embrace "Good Enough" for Your First Build

Your first prototype won't look like Celeste. That's okay. Placeholder art, basic sound effects, rough code√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùall of that is perfectly fine. Polish comes later. Functionality comes first.

Why: Perfectionism kills beginner projects. Ship rough, iterate often.

---

Tip 3: Playtest Early and Often

As soon as you have something playable (even if it's just moving left and right), have someone else try it. Watch them play. Don't explain anything√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùjust observe.

Why: You'll discover issues you'd never notice on your own. Players see your game with fresh eyes.

---

Tip 4: Don't Be Afraid to Revise Your GDD

Remember: the GDD is a living document. If a mechanic doesn't feel fun when you build it, change it. If a progression structure isn't working, adjust it. Design is iterative.

Why: No plan survives contact with reality. Flexibility is a strength, not a weakness.

---

Tip 5: Celebrate Small Wins

Did your character jump for the first time? Celebrate. Did you finish your first level? Celebrate. Game development is a marathon, and small wins keep you motivated.

Why: Progress is incremental. Acknowledging it fuels momentum.

Quick Reference: Chapter 2 Key Takeaways

Before moving on, here's a summary of Chapter 2's core lessons:

Core vs. Secondary Mechanics (2.1):
  • Core mechanics (2-4) are essential, repeated constantly. Build these first.
  • Secondary mechanics add depth but aren't required. Add after core is solid.
Player Agency (2.2):
  • Agency is the degree to which players make meaningful choices.
  • High agency = sandbox freedom. Low agency = guided experience. Both are valid.
  • Four decision types: Tactical, Strategic, Moral/Narrative, Creative.
Challenge & Progression (2.3):
  • Keep players in flow state: Challenge √¢‚Ä∞ÀÜ Skill.
  • Difficulty curve: Gradual upward slope, avoid spikes.
  • Progression types: Level-based, Skill-based, Unlock-based.
Feedback & Rewards (2.4):
  • Three feedback layers: Immediate (seconds), Short-term (minutes), Long-term (hours).
  • Intrinsic rewards (mastery, story) are deeper than extrinsic (points, collectibles).
  • Avoid grinding by making core loop fun.
Game Design Documents (2.5):
  • GDDs are blueprints. Beginner GDDs should be 2-10 pages, not 50+.
  • Living documents√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùthey evolve as you develop.
  • Balance writing (plan mechanics) with prototyping (test feel).
