Common Questions Before Moving Forward

"My concept feels too simple. Should I add more?"

Answer: NO. Simple is good for a first project. Complexity can come later. A simple, well-executed game beats a complex, half-finished one every time.

Remember: Portal's core concept is "solve puzzles with a portal gun." Celeste's is "climb the mountain." Tetris is "fit blocks together." Simple ‚â† bad.

"My concept keeps changing. Is that normal?"

Answer: Absolutely. Your concept will evolve as you prototype, playtest, and learn. That's not failure‚Äîthat's iteration, and iteration is how great games are made.

Update your Game Concept Document whenever something significant changes. Treat it as a living document, not a contract.

"I don't like my concept anymore. Should I start over?"

Answer: Be careful. It's easy to fall into the trap of endlessly restarting. Ask yourself:

  • Is the concept fundamentally broken? (unclear loop, no goal, mechanics don't work)
    • If yes, revise or start fresh.
  • Or am I just scared/overwhelmed and looking for an excuse to avoid moving forward?
    • If this is the case, push through. You'll learn more by finishing something "imperfect" than by restarting endlessly.

Set a rule: You're committing to this concept through Chapter 2. After that, if you still hate it, reconsider. But give it a real chance first.

"My game sounds like other games. Is that okay?"

Answer: YES. Especially for your first project. You're learning, not inventing the medium. Many successful devs started by cloning games they loved.

Remember: Stardew Valley was inspired by Harvest Moon. Celeste was inspired by Super Meat Boy. Hollow Knight was inspired by Dark Souls and Metroid. Originality comes from execution and personal touches, not from having a 100% unique concept.

Preview of Chapter 2: Game Mechanics & Design

üéÆ What's Next?

In Chapter 1, you focused on the big picture: concept, story, character, world. You answered "What is my game?"

In Chapter 2, we're going to zoom in and get tactical. You'll learn:

  • What are core mechanics vs. secondary mechanics? (and how to prioritize them)
  • How do you design player agency? (making players feel in control and impactful)
  • What is challenge, and how do you balance it? (making games hard but fair)
  • What are feedback loops? (how games reward players and keep them engaged)
  • How do you create a simplified Game Design Document (GDD)? (translating your concept into implementable mechanics)

By the end of Chapter 2, you'll have:

  • ‚úÖ 3 clearly defined core mechanics for your game
  • ‚úÖ A plan for how difficulty escalates
  • ‚úÖ A simplified GDD that maps out your game's mechanical structure

If Chapter 1 was about vision, Chapter 2 is about execution. You'll take your concept and turn it into something buildable.

How to Prepare for Chapter 2

1. Keep your Game Concept Document handy. You'll reference it constantly. Every mechanic you design must serve your core loop and concept.

2. Play games critically. Pick 2-3 games (ideally in your genre) and play them with a designer's eye:

  • What are the core mechanics?
  • How does difficulty ramp up?
  • What makes you want to keep playing?
  • What frustrates you, and why?

Take notes. You'll apply these observations to your own design.

3. Don't overthink yet. Chapter 2 will guide you step-by-step. You don't need to have all the answers now. Just come prepared with your concept and a willingness to iterate.

Tips for Staying Motivated

Game development is a marathon, not a sprint. Here are some strategies to keep momentum:

Break it Into Small Wins

Don't think "I need to finish a whole game." Think "Today, I'm designing one mechanic" or "This week, I'm prototyping one level." Small wins build confidence and prevent overwhelm.

Set a Schedule

Even 30 minutes a day adds up. Consistency beats intensity. Block out time in your calendar for game dev like you would for any other commitment.

Share Your Progress

Post screenshots, GIFs, or concept art to social media, Reddit, or Discord. The act of sharing creates accountability, and positive feedback fuels motivation.

Don't Compare Yourself to Pros

Remember: every incredible indie game you admire started as a messy prototype made by someone learning as they went. You're on that same path. Be patient with yourself.

Celebrate Milestones

Finished your concept? Celebrate. Designed your first mechanic? Celebrate. Completed a prototype? CELEBRATE. Game dev is hard‚Äîacknowledge your progress.

Keep a dev journal. Write down what you learned, what went well, and what challenged you. Months from now, you'll look back and be amazed at how much you've grown.
