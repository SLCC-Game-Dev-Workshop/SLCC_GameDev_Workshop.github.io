Introduction to Game Concepts and Genres

~10 min read
2,494 words
Learning Goal

Understand what makes a strong game concept and explore the major game genres to find what excites you.

What Is a Game Concept, Anyway?

Here's the thing: when you're just starting out in game development, one of the scariest moments is staring at a blank page and thinking, "What game should I make?" You might feel like you need a fully-fleshed-out masterpiece with detailed mechanics, complex storylines, and groundbreaking innovation. Spoiler alert: you absolutely don't.

A game concept isn't your complete design document. It's not every mechanic, every level, or every line of dialogue. Think of it as your elevator pitch‚Äîthe core idea that captures what your game is about in one or two sentences. For example:

  • Portal: "A puzzle game where you solve challenges using a gun that creates teleportation portals."
  • Celeste: "A challenging platformer about climbing a mountain while dealing with anxiety and self-doubt."
  • Stardew Valley: "A farming simulation where you restore your grandfather's old farm and build relationships with townsfolk."

See the pattern? These are simple, clear, and immediately give you a sense of what the game is. Your first concept doesn't need to be revolutionary‚Äîit just needs to be clear and exciting to you. That excitement will fuel you through the learning process.

If you can't explain your game concept to a friend in 30 seconds, it might be too complex. Simplicity is your friend when you're starting out!

Why Genres Matter (And Why They Don't)

Before we dive into your own concept, let's talk about game genres. Genres are basically categories that group games by their core gameplay style. Think of them like music genres‚Äîjust as "rock" tells you something about what a song might sound like, "platformer" tells you something about what a game might play like.

Here's why understanding genres is useful for beginners:

  1. They give you a starting point. Instead of "I want to make a game," you can say "I want to make a puzzle game," which immediately narrows your focus.
  2. They help you study similar games. If you're making a platformer, you can play and analyze other platformers to understand what works.
  3. They set player expectations. Players understand genres, so saying "it's a roguelike" instantly communicates certain mechanics.

But here's the important part: genres aren't prison cells. You're not locked into one category forever. Some of the most interesting games blend genres (think Undertale mixing RPG with bullet-hell mechanics, or Portal combining puzzles with first-person action). For your first project, though, picking one primary genre and keeping things simple will help you actually finish something.

The Major Game Genres (Your Playground)

Let's walk through the major genres you'll encounter. As you read each one, pay attention to which ones make you think, "Ooh, I'd love to try making that!"

Platformer

Core Focus: Precise movement, jumping, navigating obstacles

Platformers are all about getting from point A to point B by running, jumping, and avoiding hazards. The appeal? Tight, responsive controls that feel satisfying to master. Think about Super Mario Bros‚Äîthe core fun comes from the simple joy of momentum and timing your jumps perfectly.

Modern Example: Celeste

Celeste is a masterclass in tight platforming. You control Madeline as she climbs a mountain, and the game constantly introduces new mechanics (dash moves, moving platforms, wind physics) that keep the challenge fresh. What makes it beginner-friendly to study? The levels are bite-sized, and each screen teaches one clear lesson. You can see exactly how the designers layer difficulty.

Why it's appealing for beginners: Platformers teach you fundamental game design‚Äîhow player movement feels, how to pace challenges, how to reward mastery. Plus, the core loop (move, jump, overcome obstacle, repeat) is crystal clear.

If you love the feeling of mastering precise controls and want to focus on level design, platformers are your jam.

Puzzle

Core Focus: Problem-solving, logical thinking, "aha!" moments

Puzzle games challenge your brain, not your reflexes. The satisfaction comes from figuring out the solution‚Äîthat moment when the pieces click together. These games can range from abstract (Tetris) to narrative-driven (The Witness) to physics-based (Portal).

Modern Example: Portal

Portal is legendary for a reason. You're given one tool‚Äîa portal gun‚Äîand the entire game is about creative problem-solving using that single mechanic. Each test chamber introduces a new wrinkle (moving platforms, energy balls, turrets) that forces you to think differently. The genius? The mechanic is simple, but the combinations are endlessly creative.

Why it's appealing for beginners: Puzzle games often focus on one or two core mechanics, which means you can keep your scope manageable. You're designing challenges, not complex combat systems or sprawling worlds. Plus, puzzle design teaches you to think like a player‚Äîwhat's intuitive? What's too hard? What's satisfying?

Don't make puzzles so hard that only you can solve them! Playtest early and watch real people struggle (in a good way).

RPG (Role-Playing Game)

Core Focus: Character progression, choice-driven narrative, exploration

RPGs put you in the role of a character (or party of characters) who grows stronger over time. You'll explore worlds, make choices, fight battles, and often get deeply invested in the story. RPGs can be action-focused (Dark Souls), turn-based (classic Final Fantasy), or narrative-heavy (Disco Elysium).

Modern Example: Undertale

Undertale is proof that RPGs don't need huge budgets or massive teams. It's a quirky, heartfelt game where your choices actually matter‚Äîyou can fight monsters or spare them, and the game remembers. The combat mixes traditional turn-based mechanics with bullet-hell dodging, creating something fresh. What makes it special? The writing, the characters, and the sense that your decisions have weight.

Why it's appealing for beginners: RPGs let you focus on storytelling and systems design (like leveling up or inventory management) rather than fast-paced action. If you love world-building and characters, this genre gives you room to flex those creative muscles.

Your first RPG doesn't need 50 hours of content. A tight 30-minute experience with meaningful choices can be just as impactful.

Simulation

Core Focus: Replicating real-world systems, resource management, sandbox creativity

Simulation games let you manage systems‚Äîwhether that's a farm, a city, a theme park, or even someone's daily life (looking at you, The Sims). The appeal? You're given tools and rules, and you create your own goals and stories within that framework.

Modern Example: Stardew Valley

Stardew Valley captured millions of players by letting them escape to a peaceful farm life. You plant crops, raise animals, fish, mine, and build relationships‚Äîall at your own pace. What's brilliant about it? The game respects your time and lets you play how you want. Want to focus on farming? Great. Want to romance villagers? Go for it. Want to explore caves? That's there too.

Why it's appealing for beginners: Simulation games teach you about systems design‚Äîhow different mechanics interact and create emergent gameplay. They're also forgiving in terms of "failure"‚Äîthere's often no way to lose, just different outcomes. Plus, players love creativity and self-expression, which sims provide.

Simulation games can spiral into complexity. For your first project, pick ONE system (farming, or fishing, or managing a lemonade stand) and do it really well.

Action/Shooter

Core Focus: Fast-paced combat, reflexes, moment-to-moment intensity

Action games and shooters are all about quick decisions, precise aiming, and adrenaline. Whether you're blasting demons in Doom or dodging bullets in Enter the Gungeon, the thrill comes from high-intensity gameplay that tests your reaction time.

Modern Example: Doom (2016)

The modern Doom reboot perfected "push-forward combat"‚Äîinstead of hiding behind cover, you're rewarded for aggressive, in-your-face fighting. Low on health? Kill enemies up close for health drops. Low on ammo? Chainsaw a demon. The gameplay loop is relentless and satisfying, built around one philosophy: keep moving, keep shooting.

Why it's appealing for beginners: If you love excitement and energy, action games deliver instant feedback. Every shot, every dodge, every explosion feels impactful. You'll learn about player feedback (hit effects, sound design, screen shake) and how to make actions feel powerful.

Action games require polished feel‚Äîmovement, aiming, and combat need to be responsive. Budget extra time for playtesting and tweaking until it "feels right."

Strategy

Core Focus: Long-term planning, resource management, tactical thinking

Strategy games challenge you to think ahead. Whether you're building an empire in Civilization, commanding armies in StarCraft, or managing a team in XCOM, success comes from smart decisions, not quick reflexes. These games reward careful thought and adaptation.

Modern Example: Into the Breach

Into the Breach is a brilliant, bite-sized strategy game where you command mechs to protect cities from giant monsters. Here's the twist: you can see exactly what enemies will do next turn, so every move is a puzzle. "If I move here, the enemy attacks that building‚Ä¶ but if I push the enemy one tile, they miss." It's chess-like strategy in a streamlined, accessible package.

Why it's appealing for beginners: Strategy games teach you about balancing challenge and fairness. They're also often turn-based, which means you're not fighting against time constraints during development‚Äîyou can focus on the logic and systems. If you love board games, this genre is a natural fit.

Strategy games can become overwhelming with too many systems. Keep your first project focused‚Äîmaybe a small tactical battle, not an entire civilization simulator.

Visual Novel / Narrative Games

Core Focus: Story-driven experiences, dialogue choices, emotional impact

Visual novels and narrative games prioritize storytelling above all else. They're often reading-heavy, with branching dialogue choices that affect the outcome. Think of them as interactive stories where player choice shapes the narrative.

Modern Example: What Remains of Edith Finch

This game is a series of short stories about a cursed family, and each vignette plays differently. You might be a cat knocking things off shelves, or a child imagining adventures while doing chores. It's emotionally powerful, beautifully told, and proves that games can be art. The "gameplay" is minimal‚Äîmostly walking and observing‚Äîbut the experience is unforgettable.

Why it's appealing for beginners: If you're a writer first and coder second, narrative games let you shine. You're not building complex mechanics; you're crafting choices, dialogue, and atmosphere. Tools like Ren'Py or Twine make this genre especially accessible to beginners.

Great narrative games understand pacing. You don't need a novel's worth of text‚Äîsometimes less is more. Focus on impactful moments.

Genre Blending: Where the Magic Happens

Here's where things get fun: genres love to mix. Some of the most innovative games exist in the spaces between traditional categories.

  • Puzzle-Platformer: Portal (mentioned above), Braid, Celeste's trickier B-sides
  • Action-RPG: Hades, Dark Souls, Diablo
  • Strategy-Roguelike: Slay the Spire, Into the Breach
  • Narrative-Adventure: The Walking Dead, Life is Strange
  • Farming-RPG: Stardew Valley (it's a simulation and an RPG!)

The beauty of blending genres is that you can take what you love from each and combine them. Love the progression of RPGs but want faster gameplay? Make an action-RPG. Love puzzle-solving but want movement challenges? Make a puzzle-platformer.

For your first project, I'd recommend starting with one primary genre and maybe adding a light touch of another. For example: "It's mainly a platformer, but you collect items that give you RPG-style abilities." That's a clear concept with a fun twist, but it's not trying to be five games at once.

Genre-blending is exciting, but be honest about scope. Combining genres means combining workloads. Start simple, prove the concept, then expand.

Finding Your Genre

So, which genre should you pick? Here's a simple exercise:

  1. Think about games you love playing. What do you enjoy most‚Äîsolving puzzles, exploring worlds, mastering combat, building systems?
  2. Think about games you love analyzing. When you play a game, what do you notice? Level design? Story pacing? Character builds?
  3. Think about your skills. Are you more comfortable with writing, visual design, or programming? Different genres emphasize different strengths.
  4. Think about your time. Be realistic. Some genres require more content (RPGs need story and progression systems, simulations need depth). Puzzles and platformers can be tight and focused.

There's no wrong answer here. The best genre for your first game is the one that excites you enough to push through the learning curve.

Don't stress about picking the "perfect" genre. Your first game is about learning the process. Many developers make games in genres they've never played before‚Äîit's all part of the adventure!

Summary

Let's recap what we've covered:

  • A game concept is your elevator pitch‚Äîa simple, clear idea of what your game is about, not a complete design.
  • Genres are categories that help you understand and communicate what kind of gameplay your game offers.
  • We explored 7 major genres: Platformer, Puzzle, RPG, Simulation, Action/Shooter, Strategy, and Visual Novel/Narrative.
  • Genre-blending can create exciting combinations, but keep your scope manageable for a first project.
  • The best genre for you is the one that excites you and matches your strengths and available time.

Remember: Your goal right now isn't to make the next indie masterpiece. It's to learn the ropes, finish something small, and have fun doing it. The game industry is full of people who started with tiny, weird, imperfect projects‚Äîand those projects taught them everything they needed to know.
