Brainstorming Techniques for Game Ideas

~10 min read
3,124 words
Learning Goal

Learn practical techniques to generate creative game ideas and overcome "blank page paralysis."

The Blank Page Problem

Let's be real: staring at an empty document titled "My Game Idea" is terrifying. Your brain might spiral into questions like:

  • "What if my idea isn't original enough?"
  • "What if it's too ambitious?"
  • "What if someone's already made this?"
  • "What if it's just... bad?"

Here's the secret that professional game designers know: everyone feels this way. Even experienced developers sit down to brainstorm and think, "I have no idea what to make." The difference is that they have techniques to push through that paralysis and get ideas flowing.

That's what this section is all about. We're going to give you four powerful brainstorming techniques that actually work. These aren't abstract "be creative!" suggestions‚Äîthey're practical frameworks you can use right now to generate solid game concepts.

And here's the most important rule: During brainstorming, quantity beats quality. Don't judge your ideas yet. Weird ideas, silly ideas, half-baked ideas‚Äîwrite them all down. You can refine and filter later. For now, we just want to get your creative engine running.

Set a timer for 10-15 minutes and commit to writing down every idea that pops into your head, even the "bad" ones. You'll be surprised what happens when you stop self-editing.

TECHNIQUE #1

"What If?" Questions

This is one of the simplest and most effective brainstorming methods: take something familiar and add a twist. You're not inventing from scratch‚Äîyou're remixing what already exists.

How It Works

Start with a game you know (it can be famous or obscure), and ask "What if [X] were different?" The [X] can be anything:

  • What if the player had a different tool or ability?
  • What if the setting were totally different?
  • What if the perspective changed?
  • What if the goal were reversed?
  • What if a restriction were added or removed?

Examples to Get You Started

"What if Mario had a grappling hook?" ‚Üí Now you're thinking about a platformer with swinging mechanics. Maybe levels are designed vertically, and timing your swings is the challenge.

"What if Tetris had multiplayer combat?" ‚Üí Boom, you've basically invented games like Tetris 99 or Puyo Puyo Tetris‚Äîcompetitive puzzle games where clearing lines sends "attacks" to opponents.

"What if a racing game happened in slow motion?" ‚Üí Suddenly you're imagining tactical racing where every decision (when to turn, when to boost) is deliberate and strategic rather than reflex-based.

"What if a stealth game let you rewind time?" ‚Üí You could experiment with routes, fail, rewind, and try again‚Äîkind of like the way Braid combined platforming with time manipulation.

"What if Pac-Man, but you're the ghost?" ‚Üí Now you're chasing the player instead of fleeing. The power dynamic flips, and you're designing predator behavior instead of evasion.

See how this works? You're not pulling ideas out of thin air‚Äîyou're taking familiar concepts and twisting one element. That twist becomes your hook, your unique angle.

Prompts to Try Right Now

Grab a piece of paper (or open a notes app) and answer these:

  1. Pick a game you love. What if the main mechanic were reversed or inverted?
  2. What if your favorite game took place in a completely different setting (underwater, in space, inside a computer, in a dream)?
  3. What if the player only had one ability instead of many?
  4. What if there were no instructions or tutorials‚Äîjust trial and error?
  5. What if time moved differently (super fast, super slow, backwards, in loops)?

Don't overthink it. Just write down whatever comes to mind. Some answers will be silly. Some will be intriguing. That's the point.

The best "What If?" questions are specific, not vague. "What if it's scary?" is too broad. "What if you're in a horror game but you can't run‚Äîonly walk slowly?" gives you something concrete to design around.

TECHNIQUE #2

Personal Experience Inspiration

Here's a truth that might surprise you: some of the best game ideas come from real life. Not because you're literally recreating your day-to-day (though you could!), but because your hobbies, frustrations, and interests give you unique perspectives.

How It Works

Think about your own life‚Äîyour passions, your struggles, your weird knowledge. Then ask: "How could this become a game?"

Games are excellent at simulating experiences, teaching concepts, or letting people explore "what if" scenarios in a safe space. Your personal connection to a subject will make your game feel authentic in ways that generic ideas can't.

Examples from Real Indie Games

Celeste was inspired by the creator's struggle with anxiety and depression. The mountain represents that internal battle, and the game's difficulty mirrors the feeling of fighting through tough times. Players connected because it felt real.

Stardew Valley was created by a developer who wanted to escape the grind of corporate work and imagine a simpler, more fulfilling life. That desire resonated with millions of burned-out players.

Papers, Please came from the creator's fascination with bureaucracy and moral gray areas. You play an immigration officer making life-or-death decisions while following tedious rules. It's compelling because the tension feels authentic.

How to Apply This to Your Own Ideas

If you have a hobby or passion:

  • Love cooking? Design a time-management cooking game where balancing orders and ingredients is the puzzle.
  • Into history? Create a strategy game set in a lesser-known historical period, or a narrative game exploring a specific event.
  • Play music? Design a rhythm game, or a platformer where sound and movement are connected (like Sound Shapes).

If you've faced a challenge or frustration:

  • Struggle with time management? Make a game about juggling tasks under pressure (like Overcooked's chaotic teamwork).
  • Dealt with feeling like an outsider? Create a narrative game about finding your place in a strange world.
  • Overcame a fear? Design a game where the player confronts that fear in a metaphorical or literal way.

If you have niche knowledge:

  • Know a lot about plants? Design a farming or ecology sim with realistic botany mechanics.
  • Studied psychology? Create a game exploring how people make decisions under stress.
  • Worked retail? Make a dark comedy game about customer service nightmares (trust me, people will relate).

The key is that you don't need to have lived something extraordinary. Everyday experiences‚Äîfrustration, joy, curiosity‚Äîcan become compelling gameplay when translated thoughtfully.

Prompts to Try Right Now

  1. What's a hobby or interest you have that you've never seen in a game? How could it work?
  2. What's something you wish more people understood? Could a game teach or illustrate it?
  3. What's a daily frustration you experience? Could it be turned into a challenge or puzzle?
  4. What's a "boring" subject that could actually be fascinating if gamified?
  5. What's an emotion you'd like players to feel? What gameplay could evoke that emotion?

Don't feel like your game has to be autobiographical or deeply personal. Even light inspiration counts. "I love coffee shops" can become "a cozy caf√© management sim." Start small.

TECHNIQUE #3

Genre Mashup

This technique is pure creative chaos‚Äîand it's incredibly fun. The idea is simple: take two genres that don't usually go together and smash them into one game.

How It Works

Pick two genres from the list we covered in Section 1.1 (platformer, puzzle, RPG, simulation, action, strategy, visual novel). Now imagine a game that combines their core mechanics. Don't worry about whether it's "realistic" to build‚Äîjust explore the concept.

Examples of Successful Mashups

Hades: Roguelike + Action RPG + Narrative

You die repeatedly (roguelike), fight through levels with combat upgrades (action RPG), and each death advances character relationships and story (narrative).

Slay the Spire: Strategy + Roguelike + Deck-Building

You build a deck of cards (strategy/deck-building), climb a tower with randomized encounters (roguelike), and every run is different.

Stardew Valley: Farming Sim + RPG + Social Sim

You manage crops and animals (simulation), explore dungeons and level up (RPG), and romance villagers (social sim).

Dead Cells: Platformer + Roguelike + Metroidvania

You run, jump, and explore interconnected areas (platformer/metroidvania) but each death resets your progress with randomized layouts (roguelike).

Notice how each mashup creates something fresh by combining familiar ingredients in new ways? That's the magic.

How to Generate Your Own Mashups

Step 1: List out genres you find interesting (even if you haven't made a game in them).

Step 2: Pick two at random (literally close your eyes and point, or use a random number generator).

Step 3: Brainstorm what the core loop might look like. Ask:

  • What does the player do moment-to-moment?
  • How do the two genres' mechanics interact?
  • What's the goal or win condition?

Mashup Examples to Spark Ideas

  • Platformer + Rhythm Game: Your character automatically runs, and you press buttons to the beat to jump, attack, or dodge. Miss the rhythm, fail the jump. (See: Crypt of the NecroDancer for a similar vibe.)
  • Farming Sim + Horror: By day, you tend peaceful crops. By night, something sinister emerges and you must defend your farm. Tension comes from balancing resource growth with survival.
  • Puzzle + First-Person Shooter: Instead of just shooting enemies, each enemy is a puzzle‚Äîyou need to figure out their weakness or pattern before you can damage them.
  • Visual Novel + Strategy: You make dialogue choices that determine alliances, then those choices affect a tactical battle phase where your allies (or lack thereof) matter.
  • Roguelike + City Builder: Each run, you start with limited resources and must build a small settlement. Randomized events (disasters, raids, opportunities) keep it fresh, and death means starting over with new knowledge.
  • Stealth + Racing: You're racing against opponents, but making noise (crashing, honking) attracts hazards or enemies. The quietest, smoothest racer wins.

Prompts to Try Right Now

  1. Pick your favorite genre and your least familiar genre. Combine them. What happens?
  2. What's a genre combo you've never seen before? Why might it work (or not)?
  3. Choose a mechanic from one genre (e.g., "jumping" from platformers) and a goal from another (e.g., "survive waves" from tower defense). Design around that.
  4. Think of a game you love. What genre would completely transform it if added as a secondary layer?

Mashups can get overwhelming. For your first game, aim for a "light" mashup‚Äîone dominant genre with a flavor of another, not a 50/50 split.

TECHNIQUE #4

Constraint-Based Design

This might sound counterintuitive, but adding restrictions often sparks creativity. When you have infinite possibilities, it's paralyzing. When you have limitations, your brain gets creative about working within them.

Constraint-based design means you deliberately limit yourself‚Äîone button, a tiny screen, only three colors, ten-second levels‚Äîand build your entire game concept around that restriction.

How It Works

Pick a constraint (we'll give you a list in a moment), then ask: "If I could only use [X], what game could I make?"

The constraint forces you to think differently. Suddenly, you're not trying to make a massive open-world epic‚Äîyou're trying to make the best possible game with, say, only the spacebar as input. That's a design challenge, and challenges breed clever solutions.

Real-World Examples

One-Button Games: Games like Flappy Bird or Downwell use only one button (tap or jump). The challenge comes from timing, not complex inputs. Simple to learn, hard to master.

Tiny Games: Game jams like Ludum Dare often have 48-hour limits, which forces devs to create tiny, focused experiences. Many iconic indie games started as jam projects (like Celeste's original prototype).

Color Restrictions: Minit is entirely black and white with minimal animation. The limitation gives it a stark, memorable style and forced the designers to focus on tight gameplay.

Time-Based Constraint: Superhot revolves around one rule‚Äîtime only moves when you move. That single constraint defines the entire game and creates a unique tactical shooter.

No Dialogue/Text: Journey has no words at all, yet tells a powerful story through visuals, music, and player interaction. The constraint forced creative storytelling.

Constraint Ideas to Try

Input Constraints:

  • One-button control (only jump, only shoot, only interact)
  • Two-button control (move + one action)
  • No keyboard‚Äîmouse only, or vice versa
  • No direct control‚Äîplayer gives orders or influences indirectly

Visual Constraints:

  • Only two colors (black and white, or any duo)
  • Pixel art limited to a specific resolution (16x16, 32x32)
  • No character animations‚Äîstatic sprites only
  • Everything drawn with simple geometric shapes (circles, squares, triangles)

Time Constraints:

  • Each level lasts exactly 10 seconds
  • The entire game lasts 5 minutes
  • Real-time progression‚Äîevents happen whether you play or not (like Animal Crossing time systems)

Mechanical Constraints:

  • Player can't jump (how do you navigate obstacles?)
  • Player can't attack (how do you handle enemies‚Äîstealth? Avoidance? Diplomacy?)
  • No saving‚Äîpermadeath or one-session game
  • Everything is procedurally generated‚Äîno handcrafted levels

Scope Constraints:

  • Single-screen game (everything visible at once, no scrolling)
  • One mechanic only‚Äîdo one thing really, really well
  • Five levels max
  • No story or dialogue‚Äîpure gameplay

How to Use Constraints Creatively

Step 1: Pick one constraint that sounds interesting (or intimidating!).

Step 2: Brainstorm how you'd design around it. Ask:

  • What's fun about this limitation?
  • What creative solutions does it demand?
  • What genre fits this constraint naturally?

Step 3: Prototype the core idea. If it's not fun within the constraint, try a different one.

Example Design Process

Let's say your constraint is "one-button control only."

  • Brainstorm: What can one button do? Jump, shoot, flap, interact, reverse time?
  • Pick a mechanic: Let's say tapping makes you jump.
  • Design around it: Endless runner where timing jumps is everything. Or a rhythm game where tapping on beat triggers actions.
  • Add a twist: What if holding the button charges a higher jump? Now you have depth‚Äîtap for small jumps, hold for big ones.
  • Define the challenge: Navigate obstacles that require precise timing and jump heights.

Boom. You've turned a constraint into a game concept.

Prompts to Try Right Now

  1. Pick a random constraint from the list above. What game could you make with it?
  2. Think about a game you love. What if you removed one of its core mechanics‚Äîhow would it change?
  3. What's a limitation you initially think would make a game worse? Could it actually make it more interesting?
  4. Combine a constraint with a genre: "A puzzle game with no text" or "A platformer with no jumping."

Don't confuse "constraint" with "lack of features." A constraint is a deliberate creative choice, not just cutting corners. The goal is to make something tight and focused, not incomplete.

Putting It All Together: Mini-Exercise

üéÆ Brainstorming Exercise

Task: Spend the next 10 minutes generating at least five game ideas using the techniques above. Use a mix‚Äîdon't just stick to one method. Write them down. Don't judge quality yet.

Prompts to get you started:

  1. Use "What If?" to twist a familiar game.
  2. Use Personal Experience to think of something from your life that could be a game.
  3. Use Genre Mashup to combine two random genres.
  4. Use Constraint-Based Design to pick a limitation and design around it.
  5. Do any technique again‚Äîwhatever sparks the most ideas for you.

Set a timer for 10 minutes. Go.

When you're done, you should have at least five rough ideas. They might be weird, half-baked, or wildly ambitious‚Äîthat's perfect. The goal isn't to find your final concept right now. It's to prove to yourself that you can generate ideas, and that the blank page doesn't have to be scary.

Save all your brainstorming notes. Ideas that don't work for your first game might be perfect for your second or third. Nothing is wasted.

Remember: Your First Game Doesn't Need to Be Original

Let's address the elephant in the room: originality anxiety. You might be thinking, "But what if my idea isn't unique? What if someone's done it before?"

Here's the truth: someone probably has done something similar. And that's okay.

Your goal with your first game isn't to reinvent the medium. It's to learn by doing. You're learning how to design, how to program, how to balance difficulty, how to playtest, how to finish something. Those skills matter infinitely more than having a groundbreaking concept.

Think of it like learning to cook. You don't start by inventing a brand-new cuisine. You start by making scrambled eggs, then pasta, then maybe something a bit fancier. Over time, your skills grow, and then you can experiment with innovation.

So if your first game idea is "a platformer where you collect coins," that's perfectly fine. If it's "a puzzle game with blocks," great. Focus on execution, on making it feel good to play, on finishing it. Originality will come with experience.

Many successful indie devs say their first games were "clones" of games they loved. They learned the fundamentals by recreating mechanics, then iterated toward originality in later projects.

Summary

Let's recap the four brainstorming techniques:

  1. "What If?" Questions: Take something familiar and twist one element (character, setting, mechanic, goal, or perspective).
  2. Personal Experience Inspiration: Draw from your hobbies, struggles, knowledge, or passions to create authentic concepts.
  3. Genre Mashup: Combine two genres that don't usually go together and explore how their mechanics interact.
  4. Constraint-Based Design: Deliberately limit yourself (one button, two colors, ten-second levels) and design creatively within that restriction.

You also learned that:

  • Quantity beats quality during brainstorming. Write down everything, filter later.
  • Constraints spark creativity by forcing you to think differently.
  • Your first game doesn't need to be original‚Äîfocus on learning and finishing something.

The blank page isn't your enemy. It's just waiting for you to fill it with messy, weird, exciting ideas. Now you have the tools to do exactly that.
