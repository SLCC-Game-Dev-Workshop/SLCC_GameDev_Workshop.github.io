Character and World-Building

~12 min read
4,213 words
Learning Goal

Learn how to create memorable characters and compelling game worlds without needing massive budgets or months of development time.

Why Characters and Worlds Matter

Here's a question: Why do you remember certain games years after playing them?

Chances are, it's not just the mechanics. Sure, tight controls and clever puzzles matter. But what sticks with you‚Äîwhat makes you feel something‚Äîis often the characters and the worlds they inhabit.

Think about it:

  • You remember Hollow Knight not just for its challenging combat, but for the haunting, decayed kingdom of Hallownest and the mysterious Knight you play as.
  • You remember Celeste not just for its platforming, but for Madeline's struggle with anxiety and the mountain that represents her inner demons.
  • You remember Undertale not just for its bullet-hell RPG mechanics, but for Papyrus's goofy optimism, Sans's lazy charm, and Toriel's motherly warmth.

Characters and worlds turn mechanics into experiences. They give context, create emotional stakes, and make your game feel like a place rather than just a series of challenges.

The good news? You don't need a massive team or a Hollywood writer to create compelling characters and worlds. You just need clarity, focus, and a few key questions answered.

Let's break it down.

PART A: CHARACTER DEVELOPMENT

The Role of Characters in Games

In games, characters serve a few key functions:

  1. Player Avatar: The character you control‚Äîyour "eyes and hands" in the game world. This is the player character (PC).
  2. Allies and Mentors: Characters who help, guide, or accompany the player. They provide information, emotional support, or gameplay assistance.
  3. Antagonists: Characters who oppose the player‚Äîvillains, rivals, or obstacles with personalities.
  4. World-Building NPCs: Background characters who make the world feel lived-in (shopkeepers, townsfolk, random encounters).

For your first game, focus on the player character and maybe one or two supporting characters. Don't try to populate an entire universe yet. A few well-defined characters beat dozens of forgettable ones.

If your game doesn't need characters (like Tetris or abstract puzzle games), that's fine! But if you're building any kind of narrative or world, even a simple character helps players connect emotionally.

Four Key Questions for Character Design

When designing a character, answer these four questions. They'll give you everything you need for a focused, effective character‚Äîespecially for a beginner project.

Question 1: Who Is the Character?

This is their basic identity‚Äîtheir role, appearance, and essence in one or two sentences.

You're not writing a biography. You're establishing the core concept: "This is a [X] who [Y]."

Examples:

  • Mario: A plumber who adventures through fantastical worlds to rescue Princess Peach.
  • Madeline (Celeste): A young woman struggling with anxiety who's determined to climb a mountain.
  • The Knight (Hollow Knight): A small, silent bug-warrior exploring a decayed underground kingdom.
  • Chell (Portal): A test subject trapped in a sinister research facility, armed with a portal gun.
  • Bit (Pixel Quest): A sentient pixel who gained consciousness and is being hunted by the system's anti-virus.

Notice how simple these are? No life stories, no complex backstories (yet)‚Äîjust a clear, immediate identity.

Your Turn: For your game, answer: "My character is a [ROLE/TYPE] who [DEFINING TRAIT or SITUATION]."

Question 2: What Do They Want?

This is their motivation‚Äîtheir goal, desire, or need. It's the driving force behind their actions.

In games, motivation often aligns with the gameplay goal. The character wants what the player is trying to achieve. This creates harmony between mechanics and narrative.

Examples:

  • Mario wants to rescue Princess Peach from Bowser.
  • Madeline wants to reach the summit of Celeste Mountain (and, deeper down, to prove something to herself).
  • The Knight wants to uncover the secrets of Hallownest and stop the infection.
  • Chell wants to escape the Aperture Science facility.
  • Bit wants to survive deletion and discover why they became sentient.

See how each want directly connects to gameplay? You rescue Peach by beating levels. You reach the summit by platforming. You escape by solving puzzles. The character's goal is the player's goal.

Your Turn: Answer: "My character wants to [GOAL] because [REASON]."

Question 3: What Stands in Their Way?

This is the obstacle‚Äîthe challenge, opposition, or barrier preventing them from achieving their goal.

Obstacles create conflict, and conflict drives stories. In games, obstacles are often literal (enemies, puzzles, hazards), but they can also be emotional or internal (fear, doubt, moral dilemmas).

Examples:

  • Mario's obstacle: Bowser's forces, hazardous levels, and distance.
  • Madeline's obstacle: The mountain's physical challenges, her own anxiety and self-doubt, and a shadowy manifestation of her inner demons.
  • The Knight's obstacle: Infected creatures, hostile environments, powerful bosses, and the mystery of what happened.
  • Chell's obstacle: GLaDOS, deadly test chambers, and the facility's security systems.
  • Bit's obstacle: The anti-virus AI hunting them, glitching programs, and hostile corrupted code.

Notice how obstacles can be external (enemies, environment) and internal (fear, doubt). Both work. The key is that the obstacle creates resistance‚Äîsomething the player must overcome.

Your Turn: Answer: "What stands in their way is [OBSTACLE]."

Question 4: What Makes Them Interesting?

This is the hook‚Äîthe trait, quirk, or emotional core that makes players care about or remember the character.

This can be:

  • A personality trait (brave, clumsy, sarcastic, curious, stubborn)
  • A unique ability or design (portal gun, double-jump, shapeshifting, invisibility)
  • An emotional core (overcoming fear, seeking revenge, protecting someone, finding belonging)
  • A visual or conceptual distinctiveness (a sentient pixel, a robot with a flower growing out of it, a knight the size of an ant)

Examples:

  • Mario: Relentlessly cheerful and heroic. Visually iconic (red hat, mustache). His optimism is infectious.
  • Madeline: Vulnerable and human. She struggles with mental health, which many players relate to. She's not a superhero‚Äîshe's someone trying her best.
  • The Knight: Silent and mysterious. Players project their own interpretations. Visually distinct (white mask, small size, lone wanderer vibe).
  • Bit: Literally the smallest character ever (a single pixel). Gained consciousness by accident. Represents creativity and chaos in a sterile, ordered system.

Your Turn: Answer: "My character is interesting because [TRAIT, ABILITY, or EMOTIONAL CORE]."

Visual Distinctiveness: The Silhouette Test

The Silhouette Test

In games, characters are often recognized by their silhouette‚Äîthe outline of their shape. If you can identify a character from just their shadow, they're visually distinct.

This matters even in simple or low-budget games. Visual clarity helps players instantly identify characters and creates iconic, memorable designs.

Iconic Silhouettes:

  • Mario: Short, round, big nose, cap with "M"
  • Sonic: Spiky hair, long limbs, pointy shoes
  • Link (Zelda): Pointed hat, shield, sword
  • Celeste's Madeline: Pink hair, backpack, determined posture
  • Hollow Knight's Knight: Horns, small body, cloak, nail (sword)

Each is instantly recognizable from their outline alone.

How to Apply This:

Even if you're working with pixel art or simple sprites, aim for a distinct shape:

  • Exaggerate a feature: Big head, long limbs, oversized weapon, unique hat.
  • Use a strong posture: Hunched, upright, slouched, floating.
  • Add a signature accessory: Cape, backpack, tool, mask, antennae.

If your character is just "generic person standing," they'll blend in. Give them one or two visual traits that make them pop.

Visual Design Quick Tips

1. Color Palette

Pick 2-3 main colors for your character. Make sure they contrast with your environments so the character doesn't get lost visually.

  • Mario: Red and blue (warm, heroic)
  • Celeste's Madeline: Pink hair (stands out against snowy mountain)
  • Hollow Knight's Knight: White mask against dark environments
2. Exaggerate Key Features

Big eyes convey emotion. Large hands show action capability. Exaggeration makes characters expressive and readable.

3. Keep It Readable at Small Size

Your character will often be small on screen. Can you still tell it's them? Simplify details. Bold shapes win.

Remember: simple character > complex character for your first game. You can always add complexity later.

PART B: WORLD-BUILDING

What Is a Game World?

Your game world is the setting where gameplay happens. It can be:

  • Literal and grounded: A farm, a city, a forest, a spaceship
  • Abstract: A series of puzzle rooms, a geometric void, a dream space
  • Fantastical: A magical kingdom, an alien planet, a digital realm

Even abstract games like Tetris technically have a "world" (a black screen with falling blocks), but most narrative-driven games benefit from a world that feels cohesive and purposeful.

A strong game world does three things:

  1. Supports gameplay: The environment is designed around the mechanics (platforming worlds have platforms, stealth worlds have hiding spots, racing worlds have tracks).
  2. Establishes mood and tone: Bright colors = cheerful, dark corridors = tense, decaying ruins = melancholy.
  3. Tells a story: Even without words, the world can communicate history, conflict, and theme.

You don't need to build an open-world epic. You need a setting that serves your game and feels intentional.

Three Key Questions for World-Building

Just like with characters, we'll keep this focused. Answer these three questions, and you'll have a solid foundation.

Question 1: Where Does This Take Place?

This is the setting‚Äîthe physical or conceptual location where your game happens.

Be specific. "A fantasy world" is too vague. "A mountain" is better. "A cursed mountain where the weather changes based on your emotions" is even better.

Examples:

  • Celeste: A mountain in British Columbia. Natural, beautiful, but increasingly surreal and abstract as you climb higher (reflecting Madeline's mental state).
  • Hollow Knight: The underground kingdom of Hallownest‚Äîa vast, interconnected network of caverns, cities, and ruins inhabited by bug-like creatures.
  • Stardew Valley: A small rural valley with a farm, a town, mines, and seasonal changes.
  • Portal: The Aperture Science Enrichment Center‚Äîa sterile, white research facility full of test chambers and hidden backstage areas.
  • Pixel Quest: Inside a malfunctioning computer system‚Äîdifferent programs (word processor, image editor, web browser, etc.) serve as distinct levels.

Notice how each setting is specific and immediately visual. You can picture it.

Your Turn: Answer: "My game takes place in/on [LOCATION], which is [BRIEF DESCRIPTION]."

Question 2: What Are the Rules of This World?

This is about internal logic‚Äîhow the world works. Does gravity exist? Can objects talk? Is magic real? What's possible and what's not?

Consistency matters more than realism. If your world has its own weird rules, that's fine‚Äîjust stick to them. Players will accept almost anything if it's presented clearly and consistently.

Examples:

  • Celeste: Mostly realistic physics, but Madeline can air-dash once per jump. This is just accepted as part of the world's logic‚Äîno explanation needed.
  • Hollow Knight: It's a world of sentient bugs. That's the premise. Bugs wield weapons, build cities, and have souls. Once you accept that, everything else follows.
  • Portal: Portals exist and preserve momentum. That's the one rule. The rest is physics-based puzzles built on that foundation.
  • Minecraft: Blocks float, you can carry infinite weight, punching trees gives you wood. The world has video game logic, and that's fine‚Äîit's consistent.
  • Pixel Quest: Digital physics. Platforms can glitch in and out of existence, data fragments can be collected, and viruses are enemies. It's not realistic, but it's internally consistent (everything follows "computer logic").

Your Turn: Answer: "In this world, [KEY RULE or LOGIC] exists, which means [IMPLICATION for GAMEPLAY]."

Question 3: How Does the World Serve Gameplay?

This is the most important question: Why does this world exist? How does it support, enhance, or challenge the player?

Your world shouldn't just be a backdrop‚Äîit should be an active part of the experience.

Examples:

  • Celeste's mountain: The environment reflects Madeline's mental state. Early levels are bright and natural; later levels become abstract, surreal, and hostile. The world IS the internal struggle.
  • Hollow Knight's ruins: The decaying kingdom creates a sense of mystery and melancholy. Exploration feels like archaeology‚Äîyou're uncovering a lost civilization. The world tells the story of collapse and infection.
  • Portal's test chambers: The sterile, modular chambers emphasize isolation and control. The behind-the-scenes areas (when you escape) reveal the facility's decay and danger. The world shifts from "safe test" to "desperate escape."
  • Stardew Valley's farm: The farm is YOUR space‚Äîyou shape it, build it, and make it yours. The world's seasonal cycle creates natural pacing (plant in spring, harvest in summer, prepare for winter).
  • Pixel Quest's programs: Each program (word processor, image editor, browser, etc.) introduces unique mechanics tied to its theme. The world isn't just flavor‚Äîit's the source of gameplay variety.

Your Turn: Answer: "The world serves gameplay by [FUNCTION]."

The "Less Is More" Principle

‚ú® LESS IS MORE ‚ú®

Here's a hard truth: your first game's world should be small and focused.

It's tempting to design sprawling continents, intricate political systems, and deep histories. Resist that urge. Scope creep kills beginner projects.

Instead, focus on ONE distinctive aspect of your world and do it well:

  • Portal: One facility. That's it. But it's memorable, cohesive, and perfectly designed for the gameplay.
  • Celeste: One mountain. But each area feels unique, and the theming is strong.
  • Undertale: One underground. But every area has personality, and NPCs make it feel alive.

For your first game, aim for:

  • One primary setting (a tower, a forest, a spaceship, a computer, a town).
  • 3-5 areas or levels with distinct visual or mechanical themes.
  • A unified aesthetic (pixel art, hand-drawn, low-poly, monochrome, neon, etc.).

You're not building Skyrim. You're building a tight, focused experience that showcases your concept.

Don't design 20 levels before building one. Create one great level that captures your world's essence, playtest it, and then expand.

Putting It All Together: Pixel Quest Example

üéÆ Pixel Quest: Character & World

Character: Bit

  • Who: A sentient pixel‚Äîliterally a single square of code‚Äîwho gained consciousness during a system glitch
  • Want: To survive deletion by the anti-virus AI and discover why they became self-aware
  • Obstacle: The anti-virus hunting them, hostile corrupted programs, environmental glitches, and their own fragility
  • Hook: The ultimate underdog‚Äîtiny, vulnerable, unexpected. Represents creativity and chaos in a system designed for order and efficiency.
  • Visual: Bright cyan/blue single-pixel sprite with glitch trail effects; contrasts sharply against dark, corrupted environments

World: The Corrupted System

Where: Inside a failing computer operating system / digital landscape

Rules:

  • Glitches create/destroy platforms randomly
  • Deleted files leave void spaces
  • Corrupted areas have visual distortion
  • Error messages appear as obstacles

Serves gameplay how:

  • Desktop Zone: Tutorial area, basic platforming
  • File System: Maze-like exploration
  • Memory Banks: Glitching platforms that vanish
  • System Core: Final challenge, most corrupted area

Visual consistency:

  • Color palette: Dark blues/blacks (corrupted) with neon cyan/pink/green (data)
  • Aesthetic: Retro computer UI meets glitch art
  • Mood: Nostalgic but unsettling

See how character and world fit together? Bit is small‚Äîworld has tiny gaps. World glitches‚ÄîBit's story is about corruption. Everything connects.

Summary

Let's recap what we've covered:

Character Development:

  • Answer four key questions: Who are they? What do they want? What stands in their way? What makes them interesting?
  • Focus on visual distinctiveness: silhouette, color palette, signature features.
  • Keep it simple: one well-defined protagonist beats a cast of forgettable characters.

World-Building:

  • Answer three key questions: Where does it take place? What are the rules? How does it serve gameplay?
  • Apply the "less is more" principle: one focused setting beats a sprawling, half-finished world.
  • Maintain visual consistency: unified art style, coherent color palette, intentional details.

For your first game:

  • One protagonist, 3-5 key traits/details.
  • One primary setting, 3-5 distinct areas.
  • Focus on what serves your gameplay and story.
  • Consistency and clarity beat complexity.

Characters and worlds don't need to be perfect. They need to be clear, intentional, and memorable. Even simple designs‚Äîlike a single pixel or a mountain‚Äîcan become iconic when executed with purpose.
