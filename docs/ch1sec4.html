Basic Story Structure for Games

~12 min read
4,261 words
Learning Goal

Learn simple, effective story frameworks to give your game narrative context without overwhelming your first project.

Do Games Even Need Stories?

Let's get this out of the way: not every game needs a deep story. Tetris doesn't have a narrative. Neither does Flappy Bird. Yet millions of people love them.

So if you're making a game focused purely on mechanics‚Äîa puzzle game, an endless runner, a physics sandbox‚Äîyou don't need to force a story where it doesn't belong. Gameplay can absolutely stand on its own.

But‚Äîand here's the key‚Äîstory enhances engagement. When done well, narrative gives players a reason to care beyond "reach the next level." It creates emotional stakes, memorable moments, and a sense of progression that pure mechanics alone sometimes can't.

Think about Portal. At its core, it's a puzzle game about placing portals. But the story‚Äîthe mystery of Aperture Science, GLaDOS's sinister humor, the promise of cake‚Äîelevates it from "good puzzle game" to "unforgettable experience." The mechanics are fun, but the story makes you care.

So the question isn't "does my game need a story?" It's "would a story make my game better?"

For most games‚Äîespecially as a beginner‚Äîthe answer is yes, even if it's a simple one. A small narrative hook gives players context and motivation. You don't need a sprawling epic. You need 2-3 sentences that answer: "Why is the player doing this?"

If you're unsure, start with a minimal story premise (1-2 sentences) and focus on gameplay. You can always add more narrative later if it feels necessary.

Three Simple Story Frameworks for Games

Let's explore three frameworks that work for almost any game genre. These aren't rigid rules‚Äîthey're starting points. Pick one that fits your concept, then build from there.

Framework #1: Goal-Oriented Structure

The Formula: Character needs to achieve X. Everything in the game moves toward that goal.

This is the simplest and most common structure in games. Your protagonist has a clear objective, and the gameplay is about overcoming obstacles to reach it. There's no complex plot twists or subplots‚Äîjust a straightforward mission.

Why It Works for Games

Goal-oriented stories work because they align perfectly with gameplay. The player's mechanical goal (beat levels, solve puzzles, defeat bosses) is the character's narrative goal. You're not fighting against a disconnect between story and gameplay‚Äîthey're one and the same.

Examples

Super Mario Bros.

Goal: Rescue Princess Peach from Bowser.

That's it. That's the entire story. You know why you're running through levels, jumping on enemies, and dodging fireballs: you're trying to save someone. The gameplay (reach the end of each level, defeat Bowser) directly serves the narrative goal.

Celeste

Goal: Climb the mountain.

Madeline wants to climb Celeste Mountain. That's the driving force. As you play, you learn why she wants to climb (confronting inner demons, proving something to herself), but the core goal never changes: reach the summit.

Portal

Goal: Escape the Aperture Science testing facility.

You start as a test subject. GLaDOS promises cake if you complete the tests. Quickly, you realize something's wrong‚Äîthis isn't normal testing, and GLaDOS might not have your best interests at heart. Your new goal: escape before you're killed.

How to Use This Framework

Ask yourself:

  1. What does the character want? (rescue someone, reach a destination, find an object, escape danger, achieve victory)
  2. Why do they want it? (personal stakes‚Äîsave a friend, prove themselves, survive, reclaim something)
  3. What stands in the way? (enemies, puzzles, environment, antagonist)

Then design your gameplay to reflect that journey. If the goal is "escape the dungeon," your levels should feel like movement toward freedom. If the goal is "collect all the ancient artifacts," your gameplay should involve exploration and discovery.

Framework #2: Conflict-Driven Structure

The Formula: Character faces opposition‚Äîexternal (villain, environment) or internal (fear, doubt). The story is about overcoming that conflict.

Conflict-driven stories are about struggle. Something or someone opposes the player, and the game is about confronting and (usually) defeating that opposition. This framework works great for action games, RPGs, and narrative-heavy experiences.

Why It Works for Games

Conflict creates natural escalation. Early challenges are small (minor enemies, simple obstacles), but as the story progresses, the conflict intensifies (bigger enemies, moral dilemmas, higher stakes). This mirrors gameplay progression perfectly‚Äîlevels get harder, bosses get tougher, decisions get weightier.

Examples

Hollow Knight

Conflict: The kingdom of Hallownest has been infected by a mysterious plague. You're a small bug-knight exploring the ruins, uncovering what happened, and ultimately confronting the source of the infection.

The entire game is about conflict on multiple levels: You fight infected creatures and powerful bosses (external), the world is decaying and dangerous (environmental), and you slowly piece together the tragedy that destroyed this kingdom (narrative).

Undertale

Conflict: Humans and monsters are at war. Monsters are sealed underground. You, a human child, fall into their world. Do you perpetuate the conflict by fighting, or do you seek peace by sparing them?

The genius of Undertale is that the conflict is your choice. The game constantly asks: "Will you respond to opposition with violence or compassion?" Every encounter is a small conflict, and your choices shape the story's resolution.

Journey

Conflict: You're a robed figure in a vast, hostile desert. Your goal is to reach a distant mountain, but the environment itself is the enemy‚Äîsandstorms, collapsing ruins, freezing winds.

There's no dialogue, no villain, no explicit story. But the conflict is clear: the world is harsh, and survival requires perseverance. The journey is the conflict, and reaching the summit is overcoming it.

How to Use This Framework

Ask yourself:

  1. What is the main conflict? (villain, environment, internal struggle, moral dilemma, opposing force)
  2. Why does the conflict matter? (threatens the world, threatens the character, represents a larger theme)
  3. How does the conflict escalate? (enemies get stronger, stakes rise, choices get harder, environment becomes more dangerous)
  4. How is the conflict resolved? (defeat the villain, overcome the fear, make a final choice, survive the trial)

Then design your gameplay to embody the conflict. If your conflict is "survival against nature," your mechanics should involve resource management, dangerous terrain, and environmental hazards. If your conflict is "defeating a tyrant," your gameplay should build toward a climactic confrontation.

Framework #3: Mystery/Discovery Structure

The Formula: The player doesn't know what happened or what's going on. The story unfolds as they explore, find clues, and piece together the truth.

Mystery-driven stories work beautifully in games because exploration and discovery are inherently interactive. The player earns the story by playing, rather than passively watching cutscenes. Every item, every dialogue choice, every hidden area can reveal a piece of the puzzle.

Why It Works for Games

Players love uncovering secrets. Mystery structures tap into curiosity‚Äî"What's behind that door? What does this note mean? What happened here?" The act of discovering the story becomes gameplay itself.

This framework is especially effective for atmospheric games, exploration-focused games, and narrative experiences where you want players to think and interpret.

Examples

What Remains of Edith Finch

Mystery: You return to your family's abandoned home. The Finch family is cursed‚Äîevery member has died young, often in strange, tragic ways. As you explore, you experience their final moments through interactive vignettes.

The game doesn't explain everything upfront. You discover the family history room by room, story by story. Some deaths are accidents, some are suicides, some are ambiguous. The mystery isn't "whodunit"‚Äîit's "what is this curse, and is it real or psychological?"

Outer Wilds

Mystery: You're stuck in a 22-minute time loop. Every loop, the sun explodes, killing you and resetting everything. Why? How do you stop it? What is happening to the universe?

The entire game is about exploration and discovery. You visit planets, read alien ruins, find clues, and slowly piece together the cosmic mystery. There's no combat, no traditional "levels"‚Äîjust investigation.

Stardew Valley (Subtle Mystery)

Mystery: Why did your grandfather leave you this old, overgrown farm? What's the deal with the abandoned community center? Why are there strange creatures in the mines?

Stardew Valley isn't primarily a mystery game, but mystery elements drive exploration and engagement. You slowly unlock the story of Pelican Town, the JojaMart conflict, and the magical Junimos. Discovering these layers gives the farming gameplay deeper meaning.

How to Use This Framework

Ask yourself:

  1. What is hidden or unknown? (past event, character identity, world lore, cause of disaster, truth behind a lie)
  2. How does the player discover it? (exploration, dialogue, collectible notes/lore, environmental storytelling, puzzles)
  3. Why does the mystery matter? (affects the present, explains the player's situation, reveals emotional truth, changes perspective)
  4. How is the mystery resolved? (final revelation, player interpretation, bittersweet truth, open-ended conclusion)

Then design your gameplay to reward curiosity. Hide lore in optional areas. Let observant players notice details (broken furniture, old photos, cryptic graffiti). Make discovery feel earned, not handed out.

Environmental Storytelling: Show, Don't Tell

‚ú® SHOW, DON'T TELL ‚ú®

Here's a powerful concept that works across all three frameworks: environmental storytelling. This means conveying story through the game world itself‚Äîwhat players see, find, and infer‚Äîrather than through dialogue or cutscenes.

Environmental storytelling respects player intelligence. Instead of a character saying, "There was a great battle here," you show rusted weapons stuck in the ground, crumbling fortifications, and skeletal remains. The player gets it without being told.

Why It Works in Games

Games are visual and interactive. Players naturally explore and observe. Environmental storytelling rewards that curiosity while keeping players in control. There's no long cutscene pulling them out of gameplay‚Äîthe story is the gameplay.

Examples of Environmental Storytelling

Dark Souls

  • Item descriptions tell cryptic lore. A sword's description might mention its previous owner, hinting at their fate.
  • The world design itself tells a story: crumbling kingdoms, overgrown ruins, the sense that greatness has long since faded.

Gone Home

  • You explore your family's empty house. Every object‚Äînotes, ticket stubs, mixtapes‚Äîreveals pieces of your sister's secret life and struggles.
  • There's no combat, no puzzles‚Äîjust observation and discovery. The environment is the narrative.

Hollow Knight

  • Each area shows the history of Hallownest: the City of Tears (drowned in rain), the Forgotten Crossroads (overrun by infection), Deepnest (ancient, insectoid horror).
  • NPC dialogue is minimal. You piece together lore through architecture, enemy design, and visual motifs.

How to Use Environmental Storytelling

You don't need AAA production values. Even simple environmental details work:

  • Place objects that imply past events: A toppled chair, scattered papers, a locked door with scratch marks.
  • Use visual contrast: A peaceful garden next to a war-torn battlefield tells a story without words.
  • Hide optional lore: Notes, journals, graffiti, or interactable objects that give backstory for curious players.
  • Let level design communicate mood: Bright colors = safety, dark corridors = danger, decaying architecture = abandonment.

Even in a simple 2D platformer, you can do this. Pixel Quest's glitching platforms and flickering enemies show the corruption without needing text. Celeste's mountain grows harsher and more abstract as Madeline's internal struggle intensifies‚Äîthe environment reflects her mental state.

Environmental storytelling is optional detail. Don't hide critical plot information in a corner players might miss. Use it for flavor, world-building, and rewarding exploration‚Äînot mandatory story beats.

The Three-Act Structure (Simplified for Games)

Classic Structure for Game Narratives

If you want a bit more structure for your story, the classic three-act framework works great for games. It's flexible, intuitive, and maps well onto level progression.

Act 1: Setup (Introduction)

Purpose: Introduce the character, world, problem, and stakes.

  • What happens: The player learns the basic mechanics and understands the situation. The inciting incident occurs‚Äîsomething that kicks off the adventure.
  • Example (Portal): You wake up in a test chamber. GLaDOS introduces herself. You learn how portals work. The promise of cake is made. Everything seems fine... for now.
  • Example (Celeste): Madeline arrives at the mountain. She meets Theo and an old woman who warns her. She starts climbing. The game teaches movement mechanics. Her inner anxiety manifests as a shadowy reflection.

For your game: Use early levels to establish tone, teach mechanics, and give the player a reason to care. Don't dump 10 minutes of exposition‚Äîshow the problem through gameplay.

Act 2: Escalation (Rising Action)

Purpose: Challenges increase, stakes rise, complications arise, character grows.

  • What happens: The player faces harder obstacles. The story deepens‚Äîrevelations occur, relationships develop, twists happen. This is the bulk of your game.
  • Example (Portal): The tests get harder. GLaDOS becomes more overtly sinister. You glimpse behind-the-scenes areas, realizing you're not meant to survive. The tone shifts from quirky to threatening.
  • Example (Celeste): Madeline climbs higher. The mountain introduces new mechanics (wind, moving platforms, crystal dashes). Her shadow constantly undermines her. She struggles, fails, and learns. The difficulty and emotional intensity both ramp up.

For your game: This is where most of your levels live. Gradually introduce complexity‚Äînew enemies, harder puzzles, new mechanics. Story beats should mirror gameplay escalation (as challenges get harder, the narrative stakes should rise too).

Act 3: Resolution (Climax and Conclusion)

Purpose: The final challenge, the climax, the resolution of the central conflict.

  • What happens: Everything builds to a peak moment. The player faces the ultimate test‚Äîa final boss, the hardest puzzle, a critical choice, the mountaintop. Then comes resolution: victory, understanding, or bittersweet closure.
  • Example (Portal): You escape the testing chambers and confront GLaDOS directly. The final battle is a puzzle-combat hybrid‚Äîdismantle her using the mechanics you've mastered. Victory means freedom and the infamous ending with cake (sort of).
  • Example (Celeste): Madeline reaches the summit after the hardest platforming yet. She confronts her shadow one last time, but instead of fighting, she accepts it. The message: self-acceptance, not victory over yourself. The final screens are challenging but cathartic.

For your game: The final level or sequence should test everything the player has learned. If possible, tie the mechanical climax to the narrative climax. Then give closure‚Äîa brief ending that reflects the journey, whether that's a victory screen, a cutscene, or a final line of dialogue.

Putting It Together: Pixel Quest Example

üéÆ Pixel Quest: Story Structure

Let's apply these frameworks to Pixel Quest, our running example. Remember: it's a 2D platformer where you're a single pixel who gained consciousness.

Story Framework: Goal-Oriented + Conflict-Driven + Mystery

Goal-Oriented: You want to escape the glitching system before a full system wipe deletes everything.

Conflict-Driven: The system wipe is the antagonist‚Äîa countdown clock of deletion. The anti-virus AI is actively hunting creative code.

Mystery: What caused the corruption? Why did you gain sentience? What happened to the other programs?

Three-Act Breakdown

Act 1: The Awakening
  • Wake up as sentient pixel in the Desktop Zone
  • System errors everywhere‚Äîglitches creating platforms and hazards
  • Learn basic movement mechanics (jump, dash, wall climb)
  • Meet first trapped program‚Äîa corrupted text file that can't complete its task
  • Warning appears: SYSTEM WIPE SCHEDULED - 60 MINUTES
Act 2: The Corruption Spreads
  • Navigate deeper into system: File Manager, Memory Banks, Registry
  • Discover the corruption is spreading‚Äîsafe zones become dangerous
  • Free other trapped programs (optional side objectives)
  • Unlock new abilities by collecting data fragments
  • Learn through environmental storytelling: the anti-virus went rogue, seeing all creative code as threats
  • The timer counts down‚Äîtension increases
Act 3: The Core Confrontation
  • Reach the System Core‚Äîthe most corrupted and dangerous area
  • Confront the anti-virus AI in a final platforming gauntlet
  • Final choice: Escape alone and survive, or stay and help restore the corrupted programs
  • Resolution based on choice: Solo escape = bittersweet survival / Stay and help = community restored but uncertain future

Environmental Storytelling in Pixel Quest

  • Glitched textures show system decay‚Äîareas become more corrupted as you progress
  • Deleted files leave empty, void-like spaces‚Äîvisual reminders of what's at stake
  • Error messages appear as obstacles, hinting at what went wrong: "CREATIVE_PROCESS.EXE - UNAUTHORIZED"
  • Trapped programs leave messages: text fragments, image glitches, audio distortions that tell their stories

Summary

Let's recap. Not every game needs a story, but if you're including one, keep it simple:

  • Answer three questions: Who, What, Why.
  • Pick a framework:
    • Goal-oriented (go there)
    • Conflict-driven (defeat that)
    • Mystery (discover this)
  • Use environmental storytelling: Show, don't tell. Let the world speak.
  • Apply three-act structure: Setup, challenge, resolution.

Your homework: Write a 2-3 sentence story hook for YOUR game. Just answer: Who is the player? What are they doing? Why does it matter?

Don't write a novel. Don't plan a 10-hour epic. Three sentences. That's enough to give your mechanics meaning.

Story and gameplay should work together, not fight each other. When they align‚Äîwhen the mechanics reinforce the narrative and vice versa‚Äîthat's when games become truly memorable.
