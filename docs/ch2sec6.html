Exercise - Design Three Core Mechanics

~16 min read
4,500 words
Learning Goal

Apply everything you've learned in Chapter 2 to design three core mechanics for your game using a simplified GDD template.

Exercise Overview

Here's what you're going to do:

  1. Review your Game Concept Document from Chapter 1 and identify your game's core loop.
  2. Identify 2-4 potential core mechanics and narrow them down to the 3 most essential.
  3. Document each mechanic thoroughly using a simplified GDD template.
  4. Consider how your mechanics interact and create interesting gameplay.
  5. Sketch progression and how mechanics unlock or combine over time.

By the end, you'll have a Mechanics Design Document√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùa focused, 2-5 page document that explains exactly what players will do in your game and how it will work.

Let's get started.

---

Step 1: Review Your Game Concept Document

Pull out your Game Concept Document from Chapter 1.6. Let's revisit the key sections:

What to look at:
  • Core Gameplay Loop: What repeating actions define your game?
  • Genre: What's expected from your genre? (Platformers need jumping; puzzles need problem-solving; RPGs need progression)
  • Main Character: What abilities or actions make sense for your character?
Ask yourself:
  • What verbs appear in my gameplay loop? (Move, jump, shoot, collect, solve, build, dodge, etc.)
  • Which verbs are repeated most often? (Those are likely core mechanics)
  • Which verbs are essential to the experience? (Remove them and the game doesn't work√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùthose are definitely core)

Example: Pixel Quest Review

Core Loop (from Chapter 1):

"Player moves, jumps, and dashes through glitching platforms √¢‚Ä†‚Äô Navigate hazards and collect data fragments √¢‚Ä†‚Äô Unlock next program/level √¢‚Ä†‚Äô Face new glitch-based mechanics √¢‚Ä†‚Äô Repeat."

Verbs identified:
  • Move (left/right)
  • Jump
  • Dash (later clarified as "Glitch-Phase")
  • Navigate (combination of movement verbs)
  • Collect (secondary√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùnot core to loop)
Essential verbs: Move, Jump, Dash/Phase

These become the foundation for Pixel Quest's core mechanics.

---

Step 2: Identify 2-4 Potential Core Mechanics

Brainstorm a list of mechanics that could be core to your game. Don't filter yet√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùjust list possibilities.

Remember from Section 2.1:
  • Core mechanics are repeated constantly throughout the game
  • They define the gameplay loop
  • Removing them would make the game not work
How many core mechanics should you have?
  • Minimum: 1 (very rare√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùthink Flappy Bird)
  • Sweet spot for beginners: 2-3
  • Maximum for beginners: 4
  • More than 4? You're probably listing secondary mechanics or overcomplcating

Example: Pixel Quest Brainstorm

Potential Core Mechanics:
  1. Horizontal movement (left/right)
  2. Jump (vertical navigation)
  3. Wall-cling (vertical navigation + recovery)
  4. Glitch-Phase (phase through walls)
  5. Dash (quick horizontal boost)
  6. Collect data fragments (gathering)
Wait√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùthat's 6! Time to filter. Analysis:
  • Movement + Jump: Absolutely core. No platformer works without these.
  • Wall-Cling: Adds depth to vertical navigation. Core for Pixel Quest's design.
  • Glitch-Phase: Thematic and enables puzzles. Core, but could be unlockable (not needed for MVP).
  • Dash: Overlaps with movement and Glitch-Phase. Could be combined or removed.
  • Collect fragments: Secondary. Optional collectibles don't define core loop.
Narrowed to 3 Core Mechanics:
  1. Horizontal Movement (foundational)
  2. Jump (foundational)
  3. Wall-Cling (depth mechanic)
Bonus Core Mechanic (Unlockable):
  1. Glitch-Phase (mid-game unlock, core to later levels)
Decision: Start with 3, add Glitch-Phase as a stretch goal after MVP is tested.

Your Turn: Brainstorm and Filter

Action: List 4-6 potential mechanics for your game. Then answer for each:
  • Is this action repeated constantly? (Yes = potential core, No = secondary)
  • Does the game work without it? (No = core, Yes = secondary)
  • Does it directly support the core loop? (Yes = core, No = secondary)

Circle your top 3 core mechanics. These are what you'll focus on.

Step 3: Document Each Mechanic Using the Template

Now let's document your three core mechanics in detail. For each mechanic, you'll answer five key questions:

---

Mechanic Documentation Template

For each of your 3 core mechanics, fill out the following:

---

MECHANIC NAME: [Clear, descriptive name]

---

Player Input:
  • What button(s) or action(s) trigger this mechanic?
  • Are there variations? (e.g., tap vs. hold, directional input)
Game Response:
  • What happens when the player activates this mechanic?
  • How does the game world react?
  • What are the mechanical details? (speed, distance, duration, cooldown, etc.)
Why Is This Fun/Engaging?:
  • What's the appeal of this mechanic?
  • What skill does it require or reward?
  • How does it serve the core loop?
Example from Another Game:
  • Reference a similar mechanic from a well-known game.
  • What can you learn from how they implemented it?
Implementation Notes (Optional):
  • Any technical considerations?
  • Edge cases or interactions with other mechanics?

---

Full Pixel Quest Example: Three Core Mechanics

Here's Pixel Quest's complete mechanics design, documented thoroughly.

---

PIXEL QUEST - MECHANICS DESIGN DOCUMENT

---

MECHANIC 1: Basic Movement (Left/Right + Jump)

---

Player Input:
  • Horizontal Movement: Arrow keys (Left/Right) or WASD (A/D)
  • Jump: Spacebar (tap for short jump, hold for high jump)
Game Response:
  • Movement: Bit moves horizontally at 150 pixels/second with smooth acceleration (0.2-second ramp-up to max speed). Can change direction instantly mid-air.
  • Jump: Tap = 3 tile-units high (enough to clear single-tile gaps). Hold = 5 tile-units high (clears larger obstacles). Jump has slight floatiness (low gravity) to emphasize Bit being lightweight (it's a single pixel).
  • Visual/Audio: Bit leaves faint RGB glitch trail during movement. Jump has "boop" sound on takeoff, soft "tap" sound on landing.
Why Is This Fun/Engaging?:
  • Foundational feel: Responsive controls are the backbone of platformers. Bit's movement needs to feel tight and precise so players trust their inputs.
  • Skill expression: Variable jump height allows skilled players to optimize (short jumps for speed, high jumps for safety).
  • Low gravity float: Makes Bit feel "light" and thematically appropriate (a single pixel wouldn't have much weight). Also gives players more air-control for precise landings.
Example from Another Game:
  • Celeste: Movement is pixel-perfect responsive with instant direction changes. Jump has variable height based on button hold. Bit's movement should feel similarly tight.
  • Super Meat Boy: Floaty, high jumps with excellent air control. Players can adjust trajectory mid-air for precision.
Implementation Notes:
  • Test jump height extensively (3 and 5 tiles are starting points√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùmay need adjustment).
  • Ensure movement feels good at 60 FPS (smooth acceleration curve).
  • Air control is essential√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùplayers should be able to steer mid-jump without feeling "locked in."

---

MECHANIC 2: Wall-Cling

---

Player Input:
  • Hold directional input (Left or Right) toward a wall while airborne.
  • Jump (Spacebar) while clinging to perform a wall-jump.
Game Response:
  • Cling: Bit sticks to wall and slides downward slowly (50 pixels/second√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùslow enough to plan next move, fast enough to feel urgent).
  • Wall-Jump: Pressing Spacebar while clinging launches Bit upward and away from the wall (diagonal trajectory, 4 tile-units up, 2 tile-units away). Can immediately cling to opposite wall (enables wall-jump chaining).
  • Visual/Audio: Clinging produces subtle "magnetic hum" sound. Wall-jump has sharper "kick" sound. RGB trail intensifies briefly on wall-jump.
Why Is This Fun/Engaging?:
  • Verticality: Opens up vertical level design. Players can climb tall structures without needing ladders or elevators.
  • Recovery mechanic: Missing a jump doesn't mean death√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùif you hit a wall, you can cling and wall-jump to safety. Feels forgiving and skill-rewarding.
  • Skill ceiling: Advanced players can chain wall-jumps to climb quickly or reach hidden areas. Satisfying to master.
Example from Another Game:
  • Celeste: Wall-slide and wall-jump are core to verticality. Bit's system is nearly identical√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùproven design that works.
  • Hollow Knight: Similar wall-cling with slower slide speed. Emphasizes methodical climbing vs. Celeste's fast-paced approach. Pixel Quest leans toward Celeste's speed.
Implementation Notes:
  • Wall-jump angle is critical: too steep (straight up) = feels weak, too shallow (mostly horizontal) = doesn't gain height. Test 45-60 degree angles.
  • Ensure players can cling to any vertical surface (not just designated "wall" objects).
  • Edge case: What happens if player clings to a moving wall? (Likely: move with the wall, but deprioritize for MVP.)

---

MECHANIC 3: Glitch-Phase (Unlock)

---

Player Input:
  • Hold Shift while moving (any direction).
  • Phase meter drains while active; releases Shift or meter depletes = phase ends.
Game Response:
  • Activation: Bit becomes semi-transparent (50% opacity) with intense RGB separation glitch effect. Trails become rainbow-streaked.
  • Effect: Bit can pass through solid objects (walls, platforms, hazards) as if they don't exist. However, moving platforms and enemies still affect Bit (can't phase through everything√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùonly static geometry).
  • Duration: 2 seconds at full meter. Meter recharges when standing on stable ground (recharges fully in 3 seconds).
  • Visual/Audio: Static hum during phase. Rainbow particle trail. Phase meter UI element pulses and drains visually.
Why Is This Fun/Engaging?:
  • Puzzle-solving: Enables creative solutions. See a wall blocking your path? Phase through it. Glitching platform about to disappear? Phase through and land safely below.
  • Thematic fit: Bit is a glitch. This ability embodies that identity√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùyou're exploiting the system's instability.
  • Risk-reward: Limited duration means players must plan usage. Do you phase now to skip a hazard, or save meter for an upcoming puzzle?
  • Sequence-breaking potential: Skilled players can phase through entire sections, enabling speedruns and alternate routes.
Example from Another Game:
  • Dishonored (Blink ability): Short-range teleport that lets you bypass obstacles. Limited by mana meter. Feels empowering and skill-expressive. Glitch-Phase is similar but focused on phasing, not teleporting.
  • Hollow Knight (Shade Cloak): Dash through enemies and hazards invincibly. Limited by cooldown. Pixel Quest's phase has similar invincibility + utility, but with meter management instead of cooldown.
Implementation Notes:
  • Meter management is critical. Too long = trivializes challenges. Too short = feels useless. Start with 2 seconds, adjust based on playtesting.
  • Visual clarity: Phased Bit must be visually distinct so players know when active.
  • Collision detection: Need robust system to handle phasing through geometry without glitching out.
  • Unlockable timing: Introduce after Zone 1 is complete, unlock at end of Zone 2. Zone 3+ levels designed with phasing in mind.

Step 4: Consider How Mechanics Interact

Great mechanics don't exist in isolation√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùthey combine in interesting ways. Let's think about how your three mechanics interact.

Interaction Questions to Ask:

  1. Can mechanics be combined? (e.g., wall-jump + dash, phase + jump)
  2. Do mechanics create interesting choices? (e.g., use phase now or save it?)
  3. Do mechanics enable emergent strategies? (e.g., unintended shortcuts or creative solutions)

---

Pixel Quest: Mechanic Interactions

Movement + Wall-Cling:
  • Players can chain wall-jumps to climb vertical sections quickly (speed-running strategy).
  • Wall-cling allows recovery from failed jumps (forgiving, skill-rewarding).
Wall-Cling + Glitch-Phase:
  • Players can phase through walls while clinging (enables secret areas hidden inside walls).
  • Skilled players can phase, then immediately wall-cling on the other side (advanced tech).
Jump + Glitch-Phase:
  • Players can phase mid-air to pass through hazards while maintaining momentum (high-skill maneuver).
  • Phasing resets fall speed (acts as a mini-air-brake)√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùcan be used to adjust landings.
All Three Combined:
  • Wall-climb section with glitching platforms: Players must wall-jump between platforms, phase through disappearing obstacles, and manage phase meter carefully.
  • This creates high-skill challenges that reward mastery of all mechanics.

Step 5: Sketch Progression

How do players learn and master your mechanics over time? Let's plan the progression.

Progression Planning Questions:

  1. Which mechanic do players learn first?
  2. How many levels/challenges to master each mechanic before introducing the next?
  3. When do mechanics combine?
  4. What's the final mastery test?

---

Pixel Quest: Mechanic Progression

Zone 1: Text Editor (Screens 1-10) Screens 1-3: Movement Only
  • Teach: Left/right movement, basic jumps across platforms
  • No hazards, no enemies√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùjust safe exploration
  • Goal: Players feel comfortable with controls
Screens 4-6: Jump Mastery
  • Introduce: Gaps requiring precise jumps, variable height jumps
  • Minimal hazards (safe landing zones)
  • Goal: Players master jump timing and variable height
Screens 7-9: Wall-Cling Introduction
  • Introduce: Vertical walls, wall-cling mechanic tutorial
  • Challenge: Climb simple vertical sections with wall-jumps
  • Goal: Players understand wall-cling and wall-jumping
Screen 10: Combination Test
  • Combine: All three mechanics (movement, jump, wall-cling)
  • Challenge: Navigate platforming section requiring all skills
  • No new mechanics√¢‚Ç¨‚Äùpure mastery test

---

Zone 2: Image Editor (Screens 11-20) Screens 11-15: Advanced Movement
  • Introduce: RGB layer switching (new environmental mechanic, not player mechanic)
  • Test: Movement + jump + wall-cling in color-shifting environments
  • Goal: Solidify core mechanics with environmental variety
Screens 16-19: Glitch-Phase Introduction
  • Introduce: Glitch-Phase unlock at screen 16
  • Tutorial: Safe environment to practice phasing (no punishment for failure)
  • Challenge: Puzzles requiring phase (walls blocking paths, disappearing platforms)
  • Goal: Players learn phase timing and meter management

  • Challenge: Timed gauntlet with enemies, hazards, phase-required sections
  • Goal: Mastery test before Zone 3

---

Zone 3+: Complexity
  • Assume competence with all mechanics
  • Challenges combine mechanics in creative ways
  • Difficulty ramps through level design, not new mechanics

Common Mechanic Design Pitfalls

Before you finalize your mechanics, watch out for these common mistakes:

---

Pitfall 1: Too Many Mechanics

Problem: You designed 6 core mechanics for your first game. Why it's bad: Each mechanic requires implementation, testing, balancing, level design, and polish. Six mechanics = 6√É‚Äî the work. Fix: Ruthlessly cut. Pick the 3 most essential. Save the rest for a sequel or secondary status.

---

Pitfall 2: Mechanics Don't Interact

Problem: Mechanic A has nothing to do with Mechanic B. They exist in silos. Why it's bad: Players learn mechanics independently but never combine them. Gameplay feels shallow. Fix: Design interactions. How can players use A + B together? If they can't, one might be unnecessary.

---

Pitfall 3: Unclear Purpose

Problem: You have a mechanic because "it's cool," but it doesn't serve the core loop. Why it's bad: Every mechanic should have a reason. If players ask "Why do I need this?" you haven't justified it. Fix: Answer: "This mechanic enables ______." (e.g., "Phasing enables puzzle-solving and sequence-breaking.")

---

Pitfall 4: Overcomplication

Problem: One mechanic has 10 rules and 5 edge cases. Why it's bad: Players won't remember complex rules. Simple mechanics with depth > complex mechanics with confusion. Fix: Simplify. Can you explain the mechanic in one sentence? If not, it's too complex. Example:
  • Too complex: "Press A to charge jump (hold 1-3 seconds for height scaling), release to jump, but if you're on ice it charges 50% faster, and if you're crouching it charges 20% slower, and if..."
  • Simple: "Hold A to charge jump, release to jump higher."

---

Pitfall 5: Not Playtesting Feel

Problem: You designed a mechanic on paper but never prototyped it. Why it's bad: Some mechanics sound great but feel awful. Jump height might be mathematically correct but feel "off." Fix: Prototype early. Get a rough version working in-engine ASAP and playtest. Adjust based on feel, not theory.

---

Success Criteria: Is Your Mechanics Design Ready?

Your Mechanics Design Document is ready when:

‚úÖ You have 2-4 core mechanics clearly documented (not 10, not 1).

‚úÖ Each mechanic has a clear purpose (you can explain why it exists in one sentence).

‚úÖ Mechanics interact in interesting ways (players can combine them creatively).

‚úÖ Progression is mapped (you know which mechanic is introduced when, and how they combine).

If you hit all five criteria, you're ready to move to Chapter 3 and start building.
